What Is Mewing?

Definition

Mewing is the practice of maintaining correct tongue postureâ€”keeping the entire tongue pressed lightly to the roof of your mouthâ€”to support proper facial structure, jaw alignment, and nasal breathing. The term comes from Dr. John Mew and Dr. Mike Mew, pioneers of orthotropics, a branch of dentistry focused on guiding facial growth through posture and oral habits.

Key Principles of Proper Tongue Posture

  • Tongue-to-palate contact (front, middle, and back)
  • Mouth closed at all times
  • Teeth lightly touching or close together
  • Nasal breathing only
  • Good body posture (spine + neck alignment)

Important Clarification

Mewing is not a miracle cure or a guaranteed reconstructive method. It is a posture and breathing technique rooted in orthotropic principles. Consistency is key, and results vary based on age and genetics.

How to Mew Properly: Step-by-Step Mewing Tutorial

Follow this mewing technique guide to learn correct tongue posture. Each step builds on the last.

Step 1

Correct Tongue Placement

The foundation of proper tongue posture is full tongue contact with the palateâ€”not just the tip.

  • Tip of the tongue rests behind the upper front teeth (not touching them).
  • Middle of the tongue presses gently upward against the hard palate.
  • The back third of the tongue should be raised tooâ€”this is the hardest part for beginners and the most important for results.
Step 2

Close Your Lips

Your lips should touch lightly without forcing them or pursing them tightly. A relaxed lip seal is essential for maintaining tongue posture.

Step 3

Keep Teeth Lightly Together

Not clenchedâ€”just lightly touching or hovering close to one another (the "butterfly touch"). This prevents jaw tension while supporting the mewing position.

Step 4

Breathe Through Your Nose

Nasal breathing supports tongue posture, increases nitric oxide production, and improves general airway health. Mouth breathing negates the benefits of mewing.

Step 5

Maintain Body Posture

Your head shouldn't crane forward ("nerd neck"). Ensure ears are stacked over shoulders and chin is slightly tucked. Good body posture reinforces correct tongue posture.

Signs You Are Mewing Correctly

  • Tongue feels "suctioned" upwards against the palate
  • Jaw feels lightly supported without clenching
  • Neck muscles relax naturally
  • You can breathe comfortably through your nose
  • No pain or excessive pressure anywhere

Common Mewing Mistakes to Avoid

  • Only pressing the tip: The back third of the tongue is crucial for effective mewing.
  • Clenching the jaw: This can lead to TMJ pain. Keep teeth lightly touching.
  • Hard mewing (pushing too hard): Dangerous and counterproductive. Focus on suction hold, not force.
  • Mouth breathing: This completely negates proper tongue posture.
  • Forward head posture: Leaning the head forward creates neck strain and undermines results.
  • Expecting overnight results: Mewing results take months to years of consistent practice.

Mewing Exercises to Strengthen Tongue Posture

These mewing exercises help train the tongue muscles needed for proper posture.

1. Tongue Suction Hold

Lift your entire tongue to the palate and swallow. Feel the "suctioned" position after the swallowâ€”hold that for 30 seconds. Repeat 10 times.

2. The "NG" Sound Exercise

Say "Sing" or "King" and hold the "NG" sound. Notice the back of your tongue lifting? That's the position you want. Hold for 10 seconds, repeat.

3. Chin Tucks

Pull your chin straight back (making a "double chin") to align your head over your spine. Hold 5 seconds, repeat 10 times. Essential for mewing posture.

4. Cheek Suction Exercise

Suck your cheeks in while keeping tongue pressed to palate. This strengthens the buccinator muscles and supports proper tongue position.

Safety Considerations

Do not push too hard. Mewing should never cause pain. "Hard mewing" is not recommended.

If you have TMJ pain or jaw clicking, consult a dentist or orthodontist before practicing mewing.

Chronic nasal blockage should be addressed medicallyâ€”mewing depends on nasal breathing.

If you have severe malocclusion, mewing helps posture but does not replace orthodontic treatment.

Children and teens should consult with an orthotropic or myofunctional therapist for guided treatment.

Frequently Asked Questions About Mewing

How long does mewing take to work?

Most people see habit changes within weeks; structural changes (if any) take months to years. It is a long-term lifestyle change, not a quick fix. Read more about mewing timelines and expectations.

Does mewing actually work?

Yes, but with realistic expectations. The principles behind mewingâ€”proper tongue posture, nasal breathing, and myofunctional therapyâ€”are supported by dental literature. Read our full analysis of the science behind mewing.

What is proper tongue posture?

Proper tongue posture means resting the entire tongueâ€”tip, middle, and backâ€”flat against the roof of the mouth (palate). The tip sits just behind the front teeth without touching them. Lips stay closed and breathing happens through the nose.

Can adults change their face with mewing?

Changes are usually mild in adults. Soft tissue improvements and better jawline definition from muscle tone are more common than bone remodeling. Teens see the most significant changes. See mewing before and after by age.

Can mewing fix a receding chin?

Not significantly. While better posture can make the chin look stronger, orthodontics or surgery are the only treatments for large structural issues.

Should mewing hurt?

No. If you feel pain, you're pressing too hard or using the wrong muscles. Relax your jaw and focus on a gentle suction hold.

How many hours a day should I mew?

Mewing is meant to be your all-day resting posture. Just like standing up straight, you should aim to maintain it whenever you aren't eating or speaking.

What are the best mewing exercises for beginners?

Start with the tongue suction hold and the "NG" sound exercise. These teach you to engage the back of the tongue. See our complete mewing exercises guide.
